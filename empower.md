# Stent Fatigue Tester

This was a project with the EMPOWER club at Cal Poly (Endeavors to Move People Onwards With Engineered Results)


Project Link [here](https://www.cpempower.com/fdps?pgid=lnkl60a5-39d1a603-d68b-4beb-9e68-67054b906b6c) on the [EMPOWER website](https://www.cpempower.com/)

#### Role: Lead Mechanical Engineer

## CAD (Computer-Aided Design) Models
#### Below are some screenshots of CAD Models I developed for this project, using SolidWorks:


![Nozzle, Version 1](/SFT_Nozzle_Edit1_Pos1) 

This was the first iteration of the nozzle that fed the water through our mock vessel, which was also a part of an accordion assembly attached with our voice coil motor. Together, the assembly expanded and contracted to continuously push water through our system at a constant flow rate and pressure. Some notable features of this preliminary design include the wide base that allows the component to be attached to our 80-20 Aluminum frame, as well as a standardized bolt pattern on the back flange for attachment to external pieces. The converging geometry leads to a narrow outlet with an integrated barb for a seamless connection with the tubing.

![Nozzle, Version 2](/SFT_Nozzle_Edit3)

The second iteration of the nozzle expanded upon the previous design, utilizing less material for a more efficient 3D print. The back flange panel has more support to withstand the vibration of our accordion assembly. Additionally, the number of screws was reduced in hopes of a better stress and load distribution.


![Nozzle, V2](/SFT_Nozzle_Edit2)

This was the final version of our nozzle. Key features to note are the sturdy frame and integrated screw holes through the converging geometry of the nozzle. This allowed for a larger entry diameter. Additionally, more intentional fillets are displayed throughout the piece, which made for an easier and more stable printing process. 

![Nozzle, Final Version](/SFT_Bellows)

This is the final accordion assembly. Acryllic plates were added and epoxied to the back plate (which attached to our motor) and nozzle, to prevent water leaking from between the screws. The accordion component itself was manufactured from neoprene bellows, which allowed for elastic expansion and contraction guided by our voice coil motor.

![Tube Support](/SFT_Tubing_Support) 

The above image shows the tubing support we utilized to keep all of our tubing at a constant level. This ensured an optimal and consistent flow of water throughout our system.

![SFT VC Fixture](/SFT_VC_fixture_blown) 

This assembly shows the fixture for our voice coil motor. The bottom and back bases allow for attachment to our 80-20 Aluminum frame, as solidifying our fixture to reduce extra vibrations was crucial for our system. The bridging component on top further constricts our motor, and the back plate provides holes for screws on the back of the voice coil, fixing its axial movement.
